# CRO Playbook — High-Ticket Residential Service Businesses
**Rock City Lighting · Little Rock, AR**
*Last updated: May 2026*

---

## Part 1: CRO Research Summary

This section covers conversion rate optimization research specific to high-ticket residential service businesses — exterior lighting, landscaping, HVAC, custom home services, pool installation, and similar categories where the average job is $2,000–$15,000, the customer is a homeowner, the sales process involves an in-person consultation, and trust is the primary conversion variable.

---

### 1. Multi-Step vs. Single-Step Forms

**The finding:** Multi-step forms convert approximately 300% better than single-step forms for high-ticket service businesses. The most commonly cited figure in CRO literature is a 214–300% improvement, and it is consistent across industries where the form asks for more than 3 fields.

**Why it works:**

- **Commitment and consistency (Cialdini):** Once a visitor has made a small first choice — like selecting "New Installation" — they have invested something. Abandoning the form now would feel inconsistent with the action they just took. Each step completed increases the psychological cost of leaving.
- **Cognitive load reduction:** Presenting 6–8 fields simultaneously triggers an avoidance response. The brain perceives the form as "a lot of work." Breaking it into steps (1 choice → 1 optional field → 3 required fields) makes each individual screen feel manageable.
- **Progress visibility:** A step indicator (dots, progress bar, numbered steps) communicates to the user that the end is in sight. People complete tasks when they can see the finish line. This is known in behavioral psychology as the "goal gradient effect."
- **Data quality:** Multi-step forms collect more accurate data because users aren't rushing to fill everything at once. The lead you get is better qualified.
- **Abandonment insight:** With a single-step form, you know someone left but not where or why. Multi-step forms let you see exactly which step causes abandonment, enabling targeted improvement.

**Optimal structure for a service business:**
- Step 1: Intent / project type selection (radio tiles, no typing required)
- Step 2: One qualifying question (optional or low-friction)
- Step 3: Contact information (name, phone, email — minimum viable)

Keep each step to a single cognitive decision. Never put two "thinking" fields on the same step.

### 2. Optimal Form Length and Field Selection

**The finding:** Every additional required field reduces form completion rate. The industry benchmark for service business lead forms is 3–5 fields total, with 3 being the conversion-maximizing minimum.

**What to require:**
- Name
- Phone number
- Email address

**What to ask but not require:**
- Project description / notes
- Best time to call
- Address (if needed for scheduling)

**What to remove entirely:**
- Last name as a separate field (combine into "Name")
- Company name (this isn't B2B)
- "How did you hear about us?" — kills momentum; use UTM parameters or a post-conversion survey instead
- Budget range — customers don't know, and it creates self-selection bias that filters out legitimate leads who are price-unaware

**Phone vs. email priority:** For residential services, phone is the higher-intent signal. Leads who provide a cell phone are far more likely to convert than email-only leads. If you must choose a single required field beyond name, it is phone.

### 3. Radio Tiles vs. Dropdowns vs. Text Fields

**Radio tiles (visual option cards):** Best for the first step. They require no typing, present all options at once, allow scanning, and feel like making a choice rather than filling out a form. Tap/click interaction feels fast and satisfying. When tile selection automatically advances to the next step, conversion improves an additional 15–30% over tiles that require a separate "Continue" button click.

**Dropdowns:** Lowest performing input type for primary choices. They hide options behind a click, require more motor precision on mobile, and don't allow scanning. Use dropdowns only for secondary, low-stakes fields where the number of options is too long for tiles (e.g., "Best time to call" with 3 options is acceptable as a dropdown; "What type of project?" is not).

**Text fields:** Required for contact information. Keep labels visible (not placeholder-only — placeholders disappear when typing starts, increasing error rates on mobile). Autocomplete attributes (`name`, `tel`, `email`) dramatically improve mobile completion by surfacing saved contacts data. On mobile, `inputmode="tel"` brings up the numeric keyboard for phone fields.

**Auto-advance on tile selection:** When a radio tile selection automatically moves the user to the next step, friction is eliminated. This single UX change has been shown to improve form completion rates by 20–40% in A/B tests. The tradeoff is that users can't review their selection — mitigate this with a visible "Back" button.

### 4. First-Person CTA Copy

**The finding:** First-person CTA button copy ("Get My Free Quote") outperforms second-person ("Get Your Free Quote") and generic labels ("Submit," "Send," "Continue") by 90% in some studies. The most cited Unbounce test showed a 90% improvement; other tests show 20–50% gains.

**Why it works:** First-person language makes the user feel like the action belongs to them. "Submit" is a command from the business. "Get My Free Quote" is a statement of the user's desire. The shift from imperative to self-expression removes the feeling of compliance.

**Hierarchy of CTA language quality (best to worst):**
1. `Get My Free Design Consultation` — first-person, benefit-specific, no friction word
2. `Request My Complimentary Consultation` — premium framing
3. `See My Lighting Options` — curiosity-driven, low commitment
4. `Get My Free Quote` — clear, honest
5. `Get a Free Quote` — second-person, still good
6. `Request a Consultation` — neutral, acceptable
7. `Submit` — generic, no benefit stated, worst performer

**For final step submit buttons:** Name the outcome, not the action. "Request a Consultation" names what happens. "Submit" names the mechanical act. "Book My Site Visit" names the specific valuable next step.

### 5. Microcopy Placement and Abandonment Reduction

**Microcopy** is small supporting text beneath or near form elements that reduces anxiety about submitting.

**Where it matters most:**
- Beneath the phone field: "We'll only call during your preferred hours." This directly addresses the fear that a salesperson will call at inconvenient times.
- Beneath the email field: "No spam. Only updates about your project." Addresses the fear of being added to a marketing list.
- Beneath the final submit button: "No pressure, no obligation." Removes the fear of commitment. This is the highest-leverage microcopy position.
- On Step 1, before the form begins: "Takes 90 seconds." Sets a time expectation. Reduces anticipatory anxiety about how long it will take.

**Abandonment triggers microcopy can prevent:**
- "They'll call me and be pushy" → counter with "We'll reach out once, at your preferred time."
- "This will take forever" → counter with a progress indicator and "Step 2 of 3"
- "I'll be put on a mailing list" → counter with explicit no-spam language
- "I'm committing to something" → counter with "No obligation"
- "What happens next?" → counter with a clear next-steps description

**Placement specificity:** Microcopy placed directly below the relevant field is more effective than a generic disclaimer at the bottom of the form. Proximity matters — the anxiety and the reassurance need to be in the same visual zone.

### 6. Premium Positioning Language

**The finding:** Language choices signal the tier of service. High-ticket residential service businesses that use premium language across all touchpoints experience higher average job values and attract customers who are less price-sensitive.

**Key substitutions:**
| Generic | Premium |
|---|---|
| Free quote | Complimentary consultation |
| Free estimate | Complimentary design visit |
| We'll call you | We'll be personally in touch |
| Send us a message | Tell us about your property |
| Book an appointment | Schedule your design consultation |
| Cheap / affordable | Tailored / right for your home |
| Get started | Begin your project |

**When to use "free" vs. "complimentary":** "Free" outperforms "complimentary" in direct-response contexts where the audience is price-sensitive or comparison shopping (plumbers, basic HVAC repair, cleaning services). "Complimentary" outperforms "free" when the brand is positioned as premium and the customer's identity involves having good taste. Exterior lighting design is a discretionary purchase made by homeowners who care about how their home presents — this is the "complimentary" category.

**Important exception:** Never sacrifice clarity for elegance. "Complimentary design consultation" is clear and premium. "Complimentary bespoke luminance experience" is pretentious and confusing. Premium language should feel elevated, not opaque.

### 7. Trust Signals: What Works, Where to Place Them, Specificity

**The finding:** Trust signals are the single biggest conversion lever for high-ticket service businesses. A well-designed site with weak trust signals converts worse than a mediocre site with strong ones. For a new business especially, establishing credibility is the primary job of the page.

**What works (ranked by impact for residential services):**

1. **Owner photo + real name:** The highest-impact trust signal for owner-operated businesses. A real face attached to a real name tells the visitor "a person is accountable for this." Stock photos destroy trust. A candid, warm photo of the owner (not a corporate headshot) is ideal.
2. **Specific social proof numbers:** "30+ homes" beats "many clients." "5-star Google rating" beats "great reviews." Specificity signals honesty — round numbers feel made up.
3. **Named local neighborhoods:** "We serve the Heights, Hillcrest, Chenal" is more trustworthy than "We serve Little Rock." Neighborhoods show you know the area specifically.
4. **Guarantees with specific terms:** "90-Day Make-It-Right Guarantee" beats "We stand behind our work." The specific timeframe is what makes it credible.
5. **Before/after photography:** Original project photos beat stock photography. Even imperfect phone photos of real work outperform professional stock. Authenticity > polish.
6. **Named reviews with specific details:** "Tim redesigned our entire front entry and pathway lighting — the neighbors stop to compliment it every week. – Sarah H., Hillcrest" beats "Great service! – S.H." Specificity is credibility.
7. **Phone number prominently displayed:** A visible phone number in the nav bar signals that a real person will answer. Businesses that hide their phone number feel like they're avoiding accountability.
8. **Response time commitment:** "We'll respond within one business day" is a trust signal. It shows you respect the lead's time and sets an expectation.

**Where to place trust signals:**

- **Above the fold:** At least one trust element must be visible without scrolling. For most service sites, this is the phone number in the nav and a brief tagline that implies local presence.
- **Adjacent to the form:** The highest-anxiety point on a service page is the moment before submitting contact information. Trust signals placed within 100px of the CTA button (reviews, guarantee badges, specific social proof) directly reduce abandonment at the critical decision point.
- **Near the owner photo:** Pair the bio with a credential line: "10+ years · 30+ homes · Little Rock native." These specifics anchor the trust created by the photo.
- **In the hero:** One specific social proof claim in the first paragraph users read. "30+ Little Rock homes illuminated" in the hero sub-text.

**Specificity vs. generality:** Always choose specific over general. "Over 30 homes" is a specific claim — it implies you counted, it implies you're relatively new (which is honest), and it reads as more credible than "hundreds of clients" would from a two-year-old business. Matching your trust claims to your actual scale is crucial. Overclaiming destroys trust when discovered; honest modest claims build it.

### 8. Social Proof: Reviews, Numbers, Specificity

**The finding:** Social proof is not optional for high-ticket services. Homeowners spending $3,000–$12,000 on exterior lighting need to see that other people like them made this decision and were satisfied.

**Review specificity hierarchy:**
1. Named review (first + last initial minimum), specific neighborhood, specific project detail, specific outcome — highest trust
2. Named review, specific project detail — strong
3. Named review, general praise — acceptable
4. Anonymous review, general praise — weak
5. No reviews — major conversion penalty

**Numbers matter:**
- "30+ homes illuminated" on a new business is honest and specific. Use it.
- Star ratings require a threshold to be credible: 4.7+ is strong. Below 4.5 raises questions.
- Review count matters: 5 reviews at 5.0 is less convincing than 23 reviews at 4.9. Volume signals real market presence.
- Embed actual Google/Houzz reviews rather than self-written testimonials where possible. Third-party validation > self-reported.

**Where to use social proof:**
- Hero section: one specific "X homes" number
- Adjacent to the form: 2–3 named, specific reviews
- Services section: one relevant quote near the relevant service
- Footer or trust bar: review count + rating

**Photo social proof:** Before/after grid of actual projects is more persuasive than written reviews for a visual service like exterior lighting. The customer imagines their home looking like the after photo. This imagination is a pre-purchase simulation — it is the closest thing to a free trial that a service business can offer.

### 9. Speed-to-Lead Statistics

**The finding:** This is arguably the most important operational CRO factor for service businesses. The first company to respond to a lead wins the job approximately 78% of the time. The impact of response delay is non-linear and dramatic:

- Lead contacted within 5 minutes: 100x more likely to qualify than a lead contacted after 30 minutes (MIT/InsideSales.com study)
- Lead contacted within 1 hour: still 7x more likely to qualify than leads contacted after 1 hour
- Leads contacted after 24 hours: conversion rate drops by approximately 90% vs. immediate response
- The average service business takes 47 hours to follow up on web leads (Harvard Business Review)

**Why it matters so much for residential services:**
- The homeowner is in a decision-making mindset right now. That mindset fades within hours.
- They almost certainly contacted multiple businesses. The first to respond establishes the baseline against which all others are compared.
- A fast response signals professionalism and operational competence — exactly what a homeowner wants in a contractor.

**Practical implications:**
- Any response over 4 hours in business hours is a significant conversion penalty
- Any response over 24 hours is likely to result in a lost lead
- Automated immediate confirmation (SMS and/or email) is not a substitute for personal contact but buys time by acknowledging the lead instantly
- An immediate automated response + personal follow-up within 2 hours is the gold standard for owner-operated businesses

**The "best time to call" field:** This field directly addresses speed-to-lead concerns. It signals to the lead that you won't call at a bad moment, and it gives the operator the information needed to reach the lead when they're available and in a good state of mind.

### 10. Phone vs. Form as Primary CTA

**When phone is the better primary CTA:**
- Urgent jobs (broken systems, damage, safety issues)
- Older demographic (55+) that is more comfortable calling
- The operator is reliably available to answer
- The service requires immediate back-and-forth to assess scope

**When form is the better primary CTA:**
- Discretionary / planning-oriented purchases (exterior lighting is this)
- The decision involves a visual component (what will it look like?) that benefits from consultation
- The customer wants to gather information before talking to anyone
- The operator cannot always answer the phone immediately

**Best practice: dual CTA, not forced choice.** Offer both. Phone CTA prominently in the nav and hero for high-intent visitors who prefer to call. Form CTA for visitors who want to start the process without committing to a live conversation. For exterior lighting (discretionary, visual, planning-oriented), form is the natural primary; phone is the secondary for direct calls.

**Mobile considerations:** On mobile, a phone number is a tap-to-call link. The friction of calling from mobile is nearly zero. This makes phone CTAs substantially more effective on mobile than on desktop. Consider showing a larger, more prominent phone CTA on mobile breakpoints.

### 11. Page Section Order for Service Business Landing Pages

The optimal section order for a high-ticket residential service landing page, based on conversion testing and user behavior research:

1. **Nav / Header** — Logo, phone number (always visible), primary CTA button
2. **Hero** — Headline, value proposition, photo/video of actual work, dual CTA (phone + form)
3. **Social proof bar** — Quick trust credibility (review count, years, homes served, neighborhoods)
4. **Form / Lead capture** — Adjacent to founder photo and bio (the "human behind the business" pairing)
5. **Portfolio / Gallery** — Actual project photos, ideally organized by project type
6. **Services** — What is offered, framed as customer outcomes not features
7. **Process** — How it works (3–5 steps); removes uncertainty about what happens after submission
8. **Testimonials** — Full-length named reviews with specific project details
9. **Service area** — Map or neighborhood list; local specificity
10. **Final CTA section** — Low-friction second call to action near the bottom
11. **Footer** — Contact info, phone, address (or service area), key links

**Why this order works:**
- Hero establishes desire (what your home could look like)
- Social proof bar provides immediate credibility before scrolling
- Form captures the high-intent visitor who doesn't need more convincing
- Gallery and services nurture the mid-funnel visitor who needs more before deciding
- Process section removes the "what happens next?" anxiety that stops conversions
- Reviews at the bottom serve visitors who research thoroughly before contacting

### 12. Above-the-Fold Requirements for Service Businesses

Every visitor's first screen (before any scrolling) must communicate:

1. **What you do** — "Residential Exterior Lighting" must be unambiguous. No clever taglines as the only description.
2. **Where you do it** — "Little Rock, AR" or specific region. Local service businesses that don't establish location immediately lose visitors who assume they're national or in the wrong market.
3. **Why you're different** — One specific differentiator. Not "quality service" (generic and meaningless). Something like "Custom design, installed by the owner."
4. **How to contact you** — Phone number visible in the header at all times, including on mobile.
5. **Credibility signal** — At least one trust indicator above the fold. Could be a review count, a specific "X homes served," a named guarantee, or the owner's name/face.

**What does NOT need to be above the fold:**
- Full pricing
- All services listed
- Full bio
- Portfolio gallery (though a strong hero image does the visual work)

**Mobile above-the-fold is different from desktop:** On mobile, "above the fold" is roughly 600–700px of content. The nav and hero headline consume most of this. On mobile, the headline, location, and phone number are the essential three. The form can be below the fold on mobile as long as a sticky CTA button or the nav CTA remains visible.

### 13. "Complimentary Consultation" as a Conversion Mechanism

**Why consultation is the right offer for high-ticket services:**

A "free quote" frames the service as transactional — the customer asks for a price, you deliver a price. This triggers price comparison and positions your quote against three others the homeowner requested.

A "complimentary consultation" or "complimentary design visit" frames the interaction as a valuable service in itself. The homeowner gets expertise, a professional assessment of their property, and design ideas — before any money changes hands. This creates:

- **Reciprocity:** When someone gives you something valuable, you feel a mild obligation to reciprocate. A genuine, thoughtful consultation creates positive feelings toward the business.
- **Foot-in-the-door:** Once Tim is on-site and has demonstrated expertise, the customer is much further along the decision path than if they had only received an email quote.
- **Qualification:** The site visit lets Tim assess the property, the customer, and the job scope before committing to a proposal. This protects Tim's time.

**For exterior lighting specifically:**
- The visual nature of the service means customers cannot evaluate the offering without seeing it in context
- An on-site consultation lets the customer imagine the end result in their actual yard
- The dark-hours walkthrough (if Tim can arrange evening visits) is an extremely powerful selling tool — standing in the dark and pointing to what could be illuminated is nearly impossible to replicate on a screen

**Framing the offer:** "Complimentary Design Consultation" is stronger than "Free Quote" because it signals that what Tim provides goes beyond a number — it's a design service. This is accurate (Tim brings expertise, not just a price sheet) and premium.

### 14. Owner/Founder Photo and Bio: Trust-Building Role

**The finding:** For owner-operated service businesses, the founder's photo is the single most important trust signal on the page. It answers the visitor's underlying question: "Who is this, really? Is this a faceless company or a person I can trust in my home?"

**What a good founder photo communicates:**
- A real person is accountable
- This is owner-operated (premium signal — they care about their reputation)
- Approachability — do I want this person at my home?

**Photo quality guidance:**
- Warm, natural lighting preferred over flat indoor lighting
- Outdoor or property setting reinforces the service category
- Approachable smile, not posed corporate stare
- Real clothing (work attire or smart casual) — not a blazer on a landscape lighting contractor
- Should feel like you'd recognize this person if they showed up at your door

**What the bio should include:**
- Full name
- Years of relevant experience (specific number)
- Local connection ("Little Rock native," "grew up in Hillcrest")
- Why they started the business (authenticity, not marketing-speak)
- Personal details that signal trustworthiness (family, community involvement, other passions)
- Credential summary line at the end: "10+ years · 30+ homes · Little Rock native"

**Placement:** The bio works best adjacent to the lead form, not buried in an "About" section below the fold. The decision to submit contact information is a trust decision. Having Tim's face and story next to the form is the best possible trust context for that moment.

### 15. Guarantees as Conversion Tools

**The finding:** Guarantees directly reduce purchase risk and therefore directly reduce form abandonment. But vague guarantees are worthless ("we stand behind our work"), and guarantees that aren't credible are worse than none (they seem like marketing fluff).

**What makes a guarantee work:**

1. **Specificity of terms:** "90-Day Make-It-Right Guarantee" is credible. "Satisfaction guaranteed" is not.
2. **Clarity of what's covered:** What does "make it right" mean? Does it mean free re-work? Refund? This can be in small print, but some detail builds credibility.
3. **Ease of claiming:** "If anything isn't right, just call me directly." Removing perceived friction from claiming the guarantee makes the guarantee itself more credible.
4. **Matching the service:** A workmanship guarantee for installation is logical. A satisfaction guarantee for a creative/design service is harder to honor and may feel hollow.

**Types that work for residential exterior lighting:**
- **Workmanship guarantee** (specific years): "5-Year Workmanship Guarantee on all installations" — strong, logical, credible
- **Response guarantee**: "If you're not satisfied with anything, I'll come back within one week, no charge" — specific, personal, reassuring
- **Satisfaction guarantee with defined terms**: "If your lighting doesn't look exactly as designed after 90 days, I'll make it right at no cost" — specific, outcome-based

**Placement:** Guarantees should appear in the trust bar near the form, not just in fine print at the bottom. The moment of conversion anxiety is when guarantees earn their keep.

### 16. Local Specificity vs. Generic Language

**The finding:** Named neighborhoods and local references dramatically outperform generic "serving the area" language for local service businesses. Visitors want to know you know their neighborhood — not just that you'll drive there.

**Why specificity matters:**
- Proves genuine local presence (not a national service with local franchise)
- Signals that you understand neighborhood-specific context (property styles, HOA restrictions, typical lot sizes)
- Helps visitors self-identify ("They serve the Heights — that's us")
- Improves local SEO when neighborhood names appear naturally in content

**What to name:** Specific neighborhoods (Heights, Hillcrest, Chenal Valley, West Little Rock, Cammack Village, Pulaski Heights, Maumelle), not just "greater Little Rock area." The more specific, the more credible.

**In copy:** "We've lit up homes in the Heights, Hillcrest, Chenal Valley, and West Little Rock" is both a trust signal and a local SEO asset. Use it in multiple places: hero subtext, about section, service area section.

**In reviews:** When soliciting reviews, ask customers to mention their neighborhood. "Sarah H. in Hillcrest" is significantly more useful than "Sarah H." for both conversion and SEO.

### 17. Hero Headline Formulas That Work for Home Services

**Research-backed headline frameworks for residential service businesses:**

**Formula 1: Problem-agitation**
"Most [homes/yards/properties] [have a problem]. Yours doesn't have to."
Example: "Most Homes Go Dark at Night. Yours Doesn't Have To."
Works because: it names the problem the visitor already has, then offers relief without overpromising.

**Formula 2: Outcome + Location**
"[Beautiful outcome] for [specific local customers]"
Example: "Stunning Outdoor Lighting for Little Rock Homes"
Works because: clear, specific, doubly local. No cleverness that could confuse.

**Formula 3: Social proof + outcome**
"[Number] [local customers] chose us to [do the thing]. Here's why."
Example: "30 Little Rock Homeowners Chose Rock City Lighting. Here's Why."
Works because: pre-qualifies the visitor ("people like me"), creates curiosity.

**Formula 4: Transformation**
"Before 8pm, your home disappears. After Rock City Lighting, it comes alive."
Works because: vivid before/after framing. Can underperform for readers who don't emotionally connect.

**Formula 5: Direct and specific**
"Custom Exterior Lighting for [City] Homes — Designed and Installed by the Owner"
Works because: no interpretation required. Efficient for high-intent visitors.

**What kills headlines for home services:**
- Excessive cleverness or wordplay that obscures what you do
- Pure adjective stacking ("Beautiful, Professional, Affordable Lighting Solutions")
- Generic benefit claims without specificity ("Making Your Home Better")
- Passive or corporate voice ("Illuminating Excellence")

### 18. What Kills Conversions on Service Business Sites

In rough order of magnitude:

1. **No visible phone number** — Signals either the business is not reachable or doesn't want to be contacted directly. Hiding the phone number is one of the fastest ways to lose high-intent visitors who prefer to call.
2. **Single-page, single-step form with 6+ fields** — The most direct conversion killer. See Section 1.
3. **No social proof or generic social proof** — "We care about quality" is not a trust signal. Real reviews, real numbers, real names.
4. **Slow page load** — Google's research shows 53% of mobile visitors leave if a page takes more than 3 seconds to load. Every additional second costs approximately 7% of conversions.
5. **No local specificity** — "Serving the area" signals a non-local business. Named neighborhoods are essential.
6. **Stock photography** — Immediately signals "not a real local business." Even imperfect real photos outperform polished stock.
7. **Generic CTA copy** — "Submit" is a conversion killer. Name the outcome.
8. **No guarantee** — Homeowners handing access to their property to a contractor are taking on risk. A guarantee that addresses that risk increases conversions measurably.
9. **No response time expectation** — "We'll get back to you" is vague. "We'll respond within one business day" is a commitment. Commitments build trust.
10. **Buried contact information** — If the phone number and form are not findable within 2 seconds of a visitor deciding they're interested, you lose them.
11. **Owner invisibility** — A service business with no owner photo or bio feels like a faceless entity. People hire people, not LLCs.
12. **No clear next step after submission** — The post-submission experience matters. "Thanks, we'll be in touch" is weak. "You'll hear from Tim personally within one business day" is strong.

### Current State Assessment

**What's already strong:**

- 3-step multi-step form — this is correct and likely already materially outperforming single-step alternatives
- Radio tiles on Step 1 with auto-advance logic — correct UX pattern
- Tim Barnett photo + bio directly adjacent to the form — excellent placement, high trust impact
- Specific social proof in form header: "30+ Little Rock homes · Heights · Hillcrest · Chenal · West Little Rock" — this is exactly right

- "Best time to call" field — reduces speed-to-lead friction by enabling Tim to call at the right time

**What needs evaluation or improvement:**

See detailed analysis below.

### Evaluation 1: Form-First vs. Calendar-First Approach

**Recommendation: Retain form-first. Add calendar as a secondary option.**

The current flow (form → Tim calls → Tim sends calendar link → lead books → Tim visits → proposal → sign/deposit) is appropriate for this service at this price point. Here's why:

A direct-to-calendar approach (Calendly-first) works well for services where:
- Scope is predictable (coaching, consulting, defined service packages)
- No triage is needed before committing to a meeting time
- The calendar booking IS the conversion event

For exterior lighting at $3,000–$10,000+, the calendar-first flow has a problem: Tim doesn't know whether the inbound lead is a qualified homeowner in his service area with a real project, or a renter in Conway asking about Christmas lights. The phone call step is Tim's triage. It is valuable.

**However:** There is a segment of leads who are higher-intent, already decided they want Tim, and find the "fill out a form, wait for a call, then get sent a link, then book" sequence to be one too many steps. For these leads, the ability to book directly is a competitive advantage.

**Recommended dual CTA structure in the form area:**

Primary flow: existing 3-step form (unchanged)
Secondary option: below the form, add a single line:
> "Ready to skip ahead? [Book a 20-minute phone call directly →]" (links to a Calendly with one timeslot type: "Initial Call")

This serves both segments without complicating the primary form.

---

### Evaluation 2: Step 2 — Is the Optional Description Helping or Hurting?

**Current state:** Step 2 is a textarea labeled "Tell us about the property — optional" with a placeholder about front entry, backyard, pool, trees, driveway.

**Recommendation: Keep it, but reframe and shorten.**

The argument for removing it:
- Optional fields introduce ambiguity ("do I need to fill this in?")
- Every step is a potential exit point
- The description doesn't change Tim's immediate next action (he calls either way)

The argument for keeping it:
- The description gives Tim context before the call, making the first conversation better-qualified and more impressive ("I saw you mentioned your pool area — let me tell you what we could do there")
- Leads who fill it in are more engaged and more likely to convert from call to consult
- The "optional" label reduces anxiety for those who don't want to bother

**The real problem:** The question framing. "Tell us about the property" is vague. It could mean physical description, neighborhood, square footage — anything. Most users stare at it and either leave it blank or write something unhelpful.

**Better framing:** Change the question to:
> "What area of your property are you most thinking about?"

And replace the open textarea with 3–5 checkbox tiles:
- Front entry / driveway
- Backyard / patio
- Landscape / trees
- Pool or water feature
- Full property

This converts an open-ended thinking exercise into a quick tap-tap interaction. It answers the same qualification question, collects useful information, and is far less likely to cause abandonment. The "step" no longer requires effort — it's 1–2 taps.

If checkboxes are too much implementation work for now: keep the textarea but change the label to "What area are you most focused on? (optional)" and shorten the placeholder to just: "e.g., front entry, backyard, pool area..."

### Evaluation 3: Post-Submission Experience

**Current state:** Unknown — need to verify what the form shows after submission.

**What the post-submission confirmation message must do:**

### Evaluation 4: The Ideal Tim → Lead Follow-Up Sequence

Given the speed-to-lead research: **the first response must happen within 2 business hours at the outside.**

**Recommended sequence:**

**Immediately on form submission:**
- Automated confirmation email to the lead with the message above
- Automated SMS to the lead (if implemented): "Hi [Name] — it's Tim from Rock City Lighting. Got your request — I'll give you a call [timing window they selected]. Talk soon."
- Automated email/SMS notification to Tim with lead details

**Within 2 business hours:**
- Tim calls at the lead's preferred time window
- If no answer: leave a brief voicemail, then send a follow-up text: "Hey [Name], this is Tim from Rock City Lighting. Just tried to reach you — no rush, but wanted to connect about your lighting project. Feel free to text me back or I can try again [timing]."

**If no response after first call:**
- Day 2: one follow-up call at a different time in their preferred window
- Day 3: one final text: "Hey [Name] — Tim again. I'll hold your spot open for a consult but won't keep bugging you. If the timing changes, reach out anytime at this number."

**After a connected call:**
- Send Google Calendar invite within 1 hour of the call
- The invite should include: Tim's phone number, property address confirmation, and a note about what to expect ("I'll walk the property with you at dusk to show you what we can do with the light.")

**If the lead does not book after receiving the calendar link:**
- One follow-up text at 48 hours: "Hey [Name], just checking in on the consult — does one of those slots still work for you? Happy to find a different time if not."

**What the sequence must NOT do:**
- Send more than 3 total contacts before getting a response
- Use automated robotic language in any outreach (all texts should sound like Tim)
- Mention price before the site visit
- Send a promotional email newsletter before the consult is booked

### Evaluation 5: Friction Points in the Current Flow

**Friction point 1: The "best time to call" is a dropdown (3 broad windows), not a specific appointment**

The lead selects "Morning (8am–Noon)" but doesn't know when Tim will actually call. Tim doesn't know when the lead is free within that window. This creates an ambiguous, call-me-when situation.

**Mitigation:** Either add a "Book a specific 20-minute call time" Calendly option as a secondary path, or add microcopy: "We'll call you within this window — usually within one business day." The clarity reduces the "I have to be available all morning" anxiety.

**Friction point 2: The "request a consultation" to "receive a calendar link" to "book a slot" is one extra step**

After Tim calls, he manually sends a Google Calendar link. This is an asynchronous exchange that introduces the risk of: lead doesn't check email, link expires, Tim forgets to send, lead picks a slot Tim can't honor.

**Mitigation:** Embed the Calendly or Google Calendar booking link directly in the automated confirmation email. After the call, Tim can say "I already sent you a booking link in your confirmation email — does [specific time] work?" This collapses two interactions into one.

**Friction point 3: The optional Step 2 (property description) may lose mobile users**

On mobile, an open textarea requiring typing is the highest-friction input type in a form. Even labeled optional, some users type a brief response and lose context (the placeholder disappears), then reconsider and abandon.

**Mitigation:** As recommended above — either replace with checkboxes, or shorten the placeholder significantly to reduce the sense of expected input length.

**Friction point 4: No "how it works" section is visible near the form**

Many visitors want to know: what happens after I submit? Do I get a price? Does someone come to my house? Is this a sales call? Without this context, some visitors don't submit because they don't know what they're getting into.

**Mitigation:** Add a 3-step "What happens next" micro-section directly below the form area:
1. We review your request → 2. Tim calls you within one business day → 3. He visits your property for a complimentary design walk

This is low-effort to implement and addresses the single most common hesitation for first-contact form submissions.

### Single Most Important Improvement Right Now

**The post-submission experience is the highest-leverage thing to fix immediately.**

All the conversion work done by the form, the trust signals, Tim's photo, the neighborhood specifics, and the guarantees — all of it culminates in the moment a visitor clicks "Request a Consultation." What happens next determines whether the lead converts to a consultation.

If the confirmation screen says something generic ("Thank you, we'll be in touch"), it immediately deflates the emotional momentum of the decision. The visitor doesn't know what happens, doesn't know when, doesn't know if they'll be called or emailed, and feels a low-grade buyer's remorse that can cause them to submit to a competitor just to feel more confident.

A specific, warm, Tim-voiced confirmation message — with his phone number, exact next steps, and genuine no-pressure language — closes the loop and creates the feeling of: "I already know Tim, I trust him, and I'm looking forward to this conversation." That is the correct emotional state for a lead heading into a consultation.

**Second most important: Speed-to-lead protocol.** If Tim is responding to leads within 30–60 minutes during business hours, he is likely winning most of them. If it's same-day or next-day, he is likely losing half or more to competitors who respond faster. The fastest possible first personal contact — even a brief text — is often the difference between a booked consult and a cold lead.
